# Mutation Analysis of TP53

The TP53 gene is one of the most frequently mutated genes in human cancers. Mutations in this gene disrupt the function of the p53 protein, which normally regulates the cell cycle, DNA repair, and apoptosis.

## Types of Mutations

Many mutations in TP53 are **Single Nucleotide Variants (SNVs)**, where a single nucleotide in the DNA sequence is altered.

Example:

Normal DNA sequence:
ATG GAC TTT

Mutated sequence:
ATG GTC TTT

A change in one nucleotide can sometimes alter the amino acid sequence of the protein and affect its function.

## Variant Classification

Databases such as **ClinVar** classify TP53 mutations based on their clinical significance. The main categories include:

- **Pathogenic** – mutation is known to cause disease
- **Likely Pathogenic** – strong evidence that the mutation is harmful
- **Benign** – mutation does not appear to affect gene function
- **Likely Benign** – probably harmless
- **Variant of Uncertain Significance (VUS)** – insufficient evidence to determine impact

This classification highlights the complexity of genetic mutations. Even small changes in DNA can have very different biological consequences.

## Cancer Mutation Data

The **COSMIC (Catalogue Of Somatic Mutations In Cancer)** database catalogs mutations observed in cancer genomes.

Analysis of TP53 mutations in COSMIC shows that this gene contains several **mutation hotspots**, meaning positions where mutations frequently occur in tumors.

Common mutation hotspots include:

- R175
- R248
- R273

These mutations often affect the DNA-binding domain of the p53 protein and disrupt its ability to regulate cell growth.

## Clinical Relevance

Certain TP53 mutations are associated with hereditary cancer syndromes such as **Li-Fraumeni syndrome**, which significantly increases the risk of developing multiple cancers at an early age.

Understanding mutation patterns in TP53 is important for:

- cancer research
- genetic diagnostics
- development of targeted therapies

## Connection to Genome Editing

Because TP53 mutations play a central role in cancer development, genome editing technologies such as **CRISPR-Cas systems** are widely used in research to study these mutations.

Researchers can introduce or correct TP53 mutations in experimental cell models to investigate how specific genetic changes affect cellular behavior.
